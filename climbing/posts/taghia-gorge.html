Taghia Gorge,
Easter 2025

Photos from Destino Climbing

My first time in Taghia Gorge with a group of 7 Spaniards. We each brought a checked bag, sharing cams, draws, and ropes. Manu, Gonzalo, Adri, Pineda, Alvaro, and I boarded a plane to Morocco. We took the short plane ride, then spent the better part of the afternoon in a taxi crossing from Marrakesh to the Taghia Gorge in the High Atlas mountains.

The final leg of our journey was a 1 hour walk up a muddy road in the rain. It had been raining an inordinate amount over the last day or so — the river had flooded onto the road and was the color of hot chocolate. We stayed at Muhammad Mesoudi's gite. They served us breakfast every morning and dinner every evening. Even a tea with bread around midday if we were resting. If we left for climbing at 5am, they'd have breakfast ready for us before we left.

We waited the following morning for the rock to dry, then set out on our first climb, Haben Oder Sein. We climbed in the shadow of enormous limestone walls, some of the most dramatic in the world. The gorge is a hidden gem — a village accessible only on foot, surrounded by walls up to 800 meters tall, hosting some of the most sustained multi-pitch climbing in the world.

The logistics of Taghia are part of the experience. Every piece of gear has to be carried in. The approach to most routes involves a steep hike in the dark, headlamps bobbing through scrub oak and loose rock. The descent is often an adventure in itself. But the climbing is worth all of it.

Over the week we climbed a mix of moderate and hard routes — everything from 6b slabs to 8a pitches. The rock quality is extraordinary: golden limestone, heavily featured with pockets and sidepulls, often vertical to severely overhung. The routes are long, the grades are stiff, and the exposure is real.

There's something about climbing in the High Atlas with a group of friends, sharing a gite, eating tagine and couscous every evening, that feels timeless. The pace of life slows. The only things that matter are the rock, the weather, and recovery. We slept hard, ate well, and climbed every day we could.

I came back humbled and inspired. Taghia is a place that rewards patience and punishes impatience — routes that look moderate from below often have cruxes hidden in the middle that require every skill you have. I left with a list of routes I want to come back for. That's always the sign of a good trip.
