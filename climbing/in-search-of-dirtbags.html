In Search of Dirtbags

A piece written for a journalism course at Colorado College in 2015. Pseudonyms are used in place of real names. Pretty cool to look at something written about climbing 10 years ago.

It's another rainy day in Kentucky's Red River Gorge. Andi and his buddies are climbing at the Chocolate Factory crag working through a new route called Swedish Fish. He's about half-way up the 110 foot climb. While cranking through a series of greasy hand holds, he suddenly slips off the rock and takes a healthy swing. He then quickly grabs the rope and yards himself up to his last high-point to figure out why he fell. Second try — he pulls back onto the sandstone face and precisely navigates the tricky moves to the next bolt.

Wasting no time, he smoothly clips the bolt, makes some quick moves, and grabs a good hold at a rest. He shakes out his pumped forearms, chalks up, and charges the final sequence. When he clips the chains, he lets out a scream of satisfaction. His partners below return the scream. This is the dirtbag lifestyle — transient, passionate, communal.

The term "dirtbag" in climbing refers to climbers who live an itinerant lifestyle centered around the sport, often living out of vans, camping indefinitely at crags, and working just enough to fund the next climbing trip. The culture traces its roots to Yosemite Valley in the 1960s and 70s, where climbers like Royal Robbins and Yvon Chouinard pioneered big wall climbing while living out of the Valley floor for months at a time.

The Red River Gorge in Kentucky has become one of the premier sport climbing destinations in the country, drawing climbers from across the continent to its overhanging sandstone walls and featured pockets. The campgrounds around the gorge are filled year-round with climbers in various states of dirtbaghood — from weekend warriors to full-time vagabonds.

Andi has been on the road for eight months. His van, a converted Econoline with a platform bed and a small kitchen, serves as both home and gear closet. He works remotely as a web developer, enough to cover fuel and food. His climbing partners rotate — some are locals, some are passing through, some have been living at the crag for years.

What draws people to this lifestyle? It's the freedom, certainly. But it's also the community. Climbing creates an unusually tight-knit social fabric — partners trust each other with their lives on a daily basis, and that trust extends off the wall. Information is freely shared. Beta flows through campfire conversations. Meals are communal.

There's also the mastery component. Rock climbing is one of the few activities where the pursuit of excellence is truly never-ending. There is always a harder route, a more committing line, a more aesthetic style. The dirtbag chooses proximity to the rock over stability, movement over comfort, experience over accumulation.

The culture has changed over the decades. Instagram has brought visibility — and crowds — to formerly quiet areas. The van life aesthetic has been commodified. Gear has become expensive. But at the core, the ethic remains: the rock is the point, everything else is logistics.

Andi clips down from the chains and lowers to the ground, already planning his next attempt on the harder project around the corner. His partners are laughing about something. Someone is starting a camp stove. The rain has stopped. Another day in the gorge.
